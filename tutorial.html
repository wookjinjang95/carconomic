Depreciation
How does depreciation work?

There is always a “golden” rule when it comes to purchasing a car: never buy it new, always buy it pre-owned. But why is this the case? The reason why people want to avoid purchasing a new car is to avoid the depreciation, or devaluation, of the vehicle. In fact, new cars depreciate a lot faster than used cars, with the value dropping roughly 20% after the first year of ownership then continuing to depreciate around 10% the following year. After 5 years or so, a car could be worth about half of its initial sticker price. Depreciation typically slows down after the 5-year mark, and essentially stops once the car reaches the tender age of 10 years [Experian article]. In this tutorial, it will explain how depreciation works and how some cars have slower depreciation rates than others. To demonstrate how depreciation works on certain cars, we will be using two models: the BMW M3 and M5.

Before starting, it will be great if we can collect data first. We will be collecting data for each BMW M3 and M5 that's currently available on the market. For example, some websites show miles and price for all BMW M3 and M5. By collecting all the information, we can see how the market behaves for those specific vehicles.

What is the straight line in the graph?
The straight line is called a linear regression, or linear model. Basically, gathering all the data (dots) in the graph, the linear regression, which is in a straight line, will show trend of the graph. This trend is basically demonstrating how much will it cost per one mile. However, this line itself is not useful This is just an ideal market where each mile will have fixed cost. As a consumer, we wish that there is no sudden depreciation and every car has a fixed depreciation rate per each mile. Life would have been so much easier. But that's not the case as we saw in previous data for BMW M3 and M5 depreciation data table. Now let's draw another line called depreciation line or logartihmic regression model.
What is the depreciation line?

As mentioned in chapter 1 about the depreciation, a car's value depreciates faster in the beginning of couple thousand miles and slows in depreciation rate as you drive more. Let's draw this depreciation line along with the straight line we drawn before in chapter 2. If you check the graph above, there are two lines that's been drawn. Red line is the straight line that we mentioned as a linear regression model. The blue line is a depreciation line. If you look at the depreciation line, we notices that, in the beginning, the line goes straight down. A lot more slope than any other miles. This shows how the depreciation rate is at highest for brand new cars.

If you go towards 60,000 miles mark on the graph, you notice that the depreication line starts to become linear and less slope. This tells us that the depreciation rate slows down as the car gets driven in a long term period. So the real question is how these two lines combined together in one graph can bring insight to us?

Insights
What insights can we learn from these two lines?

Please remember that these insights are just general theory. There is no evidenence that supports these theories or hypothesis. These are the below insights that Carconomic believes. We will think two different perspectives, a consumer's perspective and seller's perspective.

Consumer Insights

  • [Consumer] Avoid Early Depreciation

    The first point is where depreciation line and straight line meets. That is when depreciation rate starts to slow down. This meams that the vehicle passed the huge depreciation rate phase. For example, BMW M5 biggest depreciation rate stops at 9000 miles with price around 88,000 dollars. This tells us that if you are interested in buying pre-owned cars, the best mileage will be to buy BMW M5, in this case, at 9000 miles.

  • [Consumer] Avoid Any Depreciation

    Second Point is where depreciation line draws becomes linear and draws above the straight line, this is where consumer will be interested in buying a car that will hold its value well. However, starting at second point, there will be maintenance cost coming in.

Seller Insights

  • [Seller] Big Market at First Point

    Assuming you are the owner of the vehicle, the owner probably lost some money due to early depreciation. It's too late as a seller to recover the money that it losts from early depreciation. At first point, sellers can sell their cars due to high demand from buyers. The demand for a car at first point is the highest compare to other mileage. It will be easy to sell the car at that first point's price and miles. Also if you already owned the vehicle before the first point, please try to sell before the first point meets or passes to avoid losing money from depreication as much as possible.

  • [Seller] Avoid maintenance cost

    Once the second point comes across, the vehicle pretty much is considered used and going through a phase where things are going to break. There is a reason why the depreciation rate slowed down and becomes linear starting at that the second point due to maintenance cost.

Combined Both Buyer and Seller Insights

  • As a buyer, you can buy the vehicle at first point and sell the vehicle at second point. This way, you are avoidiing the early sharp depreciation rate and sell the vehicle before maintenance becomes a problem.

We see that there are different insights from different perspective from buyers and sellers. But one graph can bring multiple insights and it's pretty much up to the user to decide which sounds better for him or her.
